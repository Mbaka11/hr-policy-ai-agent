# Behavioural Competencies at McGill University

## Purpose

Behavioural Competencies provide a **common language and frame of reference** for attracting, retaining, developing, and recognizing the best talent at McGill. They were identified through:

- Interviews with McGill senior management
- Staff focus groups
- Benchmarking research with peer institutions

They represent the most vital behavioural skills for McGill's continuous institutional growth.

## The 7 Behavioural Competencies

McGill has defined seven core behavioural competencies that apply to all staff:

1. **Change Agility** — Adapts effectively to evolving priorities, processes, and organizational changes
2. **Resourcefulness** — Finds creative and effective ways to address challenges and achieve objectives with available resources
3. **Teaming** — Collaborates constructively with colleagues across units and levels to achieve shared goals
4. **Managerial Courage** — Addresses difficult situations with honesty, transparency, and decisiveness (particularly for supervisors)
5. **Self-Awareness and Management** — Understands personal strengths and development areas; regulates behaviour and emotions in a professional context
6. **Client Service Orientation** — Focuses on meeting the needs of internal and external clients with responsiveness and quality
7. **Performance Orientation** — Takes initiative to deliver high-quality results, holds oneself accountable, and pursues continuous improvement

## Proficiency Levels by Job Grade

Expected proficiency levels for each competency are **linked to job grades** according to:

- **Job family**: HRM (Human Resources Management), IST (Information Systems and Technology), ADM (Administrative), and others
- **Job level**: M1, M2, M3, M4 (increasing seniority/scope)

Higher-grade positions (M3, M4) are expected to demonstrate higher proficiency — e.g., a Director is expected to model Change Agility more broadly than an administrative coordinator.

## How Competencies Are Assessed in Performance Dialogue

During the annual Performance Dialogue (PD) session:

- The employee identifies **which competencies they are meeting** and which need further development
- The supervisor provides feedback on behavioural competency proficiency
- Both parties discuss **development actions** to strengthen weaker competencies

Competencies are discussed within the context of the employee's Individual Work Objectives — strong competencies help achieve objectives; gaps are addressed through training plans.

If an employee surpasses expectations in a competency, this should be noted and can be used to inform career development discussions and merit considerations.
