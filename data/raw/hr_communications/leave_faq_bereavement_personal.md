## Part 1: Bereavement Leave

**Q: My father-in-law just passed away. How many days am I entitled to?**
A: For the death of a **spouse's parent**, you are entitled to **3 days of paid bereavement leave**. This applies to your biological parent-in-law, step-parent-in-law, or adoptive parent-in-law. Entitlements by relationship:

| Relationship                                     | Paid Days |
| ------------------------------------------------ | --------- |
| Spouse / Common-law partner (including same-sex) | 5 days    |
| Parent (including step/adoptive)                 | 5 days    |
| Child (including step/adopted)                   | 5 days    |
| Sibling                                          | 3 days    |
| Spouse's parent                                  | 3 days    |
| Grandparent                                      | 3 days    |
| Grandchild                                       | 3 days    |
| Sibling's spouse                                 | 2 days    |
| Child's spouse                                   | 2 days    |
| Grandparent's spouse                             | 1 day     |
| Aunt/Uncle                                       | 1 day     |
| Niece/Nephew                                     | 1 day     |

**Q: The funeral is in Vancouver and I'm in Montreal. Do I get extra days for travel?**
A: Yes. If the funeral or memorial service requires travel of more than **160 km from your normal place of work**, you are entitled to **1 additional paid day** for travel, on top of the standard entitlement.

**Q: My bereavement leave falls over a weekend. Do I lose those days?**
A: Bereavement leave days are counted as **working days**, not calendar days. If a weekend falls within the bereavement period, those days are not counted. For example, if your entitlement is 5 days and the death occurs on a Thursday, your leave covers Thursday and Friday of that week, plus Monday–Wednesday of the following week.

**Q: I need more than the 5 days to manage the estate and travel internationally. Can I get more time?**
A: You may request **additional unpaid leave** for up to 15 calendar days following the death of a close family member (immediate family). This is not automatic — discuss with your supervisor and contact your HR Advisor to put it in writing. After the 15-day unpaid window, you may also apply to use accrued vacation credits.

**Q: My colleague died — are bereavement days available for deaths of non-family members?**
A: Formal paid bereavement leave is only available for family members as listed in the policy. For the death of a close friend or colleague, you may use **personal leave days** (see below) or discuss with your supervisor about taking a half-day or full day. Many supervisors will accommodate this informally.

## Part 2: Personal Leave Days

**Q: How many personal days do I have per year?**
A: You are entitled to **2 personal days per year**, to be taken within the current calendar year (January 1 – December 31). Personal days do not accrue — unused personal days do not carry over to the next year.

**Q: Can I take half a personal day?**
A: Yes — the minimum increment is **one half-day (half a working day)**. You cannot take personal leave in shorter increments.

**Q: Do I need to give a reason for taking a personal day?**
A: No. Personal leave does not require justification. However, it does require your supervisor's approval for scheduling purposes and **may not be used to extend a vacation or statutory holiday**. For example, you cannot use a personal day on the Friday before a Monday holiday to create a 4-day weekend, if that is its primary purpose.

**Q: I only started working at McGill in October — am I still entitled to 2 personal days?**
A: Personal days are not prorated based on start date. If you start after January 1 but before December 31, you have access to both personal days for the remainder of the year.

## Part 3: Other Short-Term Leaves

**Q: I have a moving day — do I get a paid day for that?**
A: Yes. You are entitled to **1 paid day per year** for moving to a new permanent residence. This applies once per year and requires supervisor approval for scheduling.

**Q: I've been called for jury duty — what happens to my pay?**
A: You receive your **full regular pay** while serving jury duty. You keep any jury duty fees paid by the court — McGill does not deduct or claim them back. Notify your supervisor and HR as soon as you receive the summons so alternate coverage can be arranged.

**Q: I have to appear in court as a witness — is that covered?**
A: If you are subpoenaed (required by law) to appear as a witness in a proceeding where you are not a party, you are entitled to **paid leave** for the duration. If you are voluntarily appearing (e.g., as a character witness), discuss with your supervisor — this may be covered by vacation or a leave without pay arrangement.

**Q: My child is suddenly sick and I have no childcare — what do I do?**
A: You can use a **personal leave day** (if you have one remaining). Beyond that, you may use a vacation day, or request **unpaid family/parental absence** (up to 10 unpaid days per year for family responsibilities). You are not required to use these in full-day increments — minimum increments depend on the leave type.

---

## Part 4: Practical Reminders

- All leaves other than unforeseeable emergencies should be **communicated to your supervisor as early as possible**
- Most paid leave types are tracked in Workday — your supervisor or HR Advisor may ask you to code your absence correctly
- When in doubt, contact your HR Advisor before your leave rather than after — this avoids payroll corrections
- If you are absent for more than **3 consecutive working days** due to illness, a medical certificate may be required
